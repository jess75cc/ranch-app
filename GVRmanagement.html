Winter feed suggestions – Austin, Nevada area

These are AI‑generated, general beef‑herd guidelines for a high‑desert winter near Austin, Nevada. Always adjust for your hay test, body condition score, and veterinarian or nutritionist advice.

Mature cows (pregnant, mid‑gestation)

  • Target intake: about 2.0–2.2% of body weight (dry matter) per day.
  • For a 1,200–1,350 lb beef cow, that is roughly 25–30 lb of good‑quality grass or mixed hay “as‑fed” per day, plus 10–15% extra if wastage is high.
  • In extended cold snaps (below about 20°F for several days), increase energy 10–20% (more hay or a few pounds of grain/cube) to hold body condition.

Bred heifers and 1st/2nd‑calf heifers

  • Heifers are still growing and need more energy and protein than mature cows at the same weight.
  • Plan on roughly 2.2–2.5% of body weight (dry matter) per day.
  • For a 950–1,050 lb heifer, this is about 22–28 lb of good‑quality hay per day “as‑fed”, plus 1–3 lb of a higher‑protein supplement if hay is mostly grass or low‑testing native hay.
  • Heifers should gain about 0.5–1.0 lb per day through winter to calve in good condition (BCS 5–6).

Mature bulls (maintenance, not working)

  • Mature bulls usually eat 2.0–2.3% of body weight (dry matter) for maintenance.
  • A 1,800–2,000 lb bull will often need around 35–45 lb of good‑quality hay per day “as‑fed”; adjust up if hay is very stemmy or weather is severe.
  • Keep bulls in moderate flesh (BCS 5–6) going into breeding; add a few pounds of grain or higher‑quality alfalfa if body condition starts to drop late in winter.

Rule of thumb: most beef cows and bulls will eat around 2–2.5% of body weight in dry matter daily in winter. Weigh a few bales and use your hay test to fine‑tune these numbers for your ranch.

AI view – best time to sell Nevada calves

Seasonal studies of feeder cattle prices show that 500–600 lb calves usually bring their best prices in late winter through spring, while prices often soften in the fall when large runs of spring‑born calves hit auctions. [web:90][web:93] For a central Nevada ranch selling 500–600 lb calves, this pattern generally favors marketing weaned, preconditioned calves from late February into May, or holding some to sell as heavier yearlings later in summer, instead of shipping the whole calf crop in October when supplies and pressure are highest. [web:90][web:93]

  • Spring sell strategy: Wean and precondition calves (VAC‑45 style program), then target late winter and early spring Superior video sales or regional auctions when grass buyers are active and calf runs are lighter. [web:45][web:52]
  • Yearling strategy: If you have summer forage, retain a portion of the calf crop and sell as 750–850 lb yearlings in late summer when that weight class often prices well relative to feed and futures. [web:93]
  • Risk note: Futures markets, drought, feed costs, and policy can change seasonal moves in any given year, so always compare current Superior summaries and local barn reports before committing to specific sale dates. [web:90][web:94]

AI market trend outlook

  • Short‑term (next 3–6 months): With beef production and cattle slaughter running below last year and feeder supplies relatively tight, the bias for good‑quality, weaned calves is generally steady to firm, with occasional setbacks after big video or regional sales. [web:53][web:89][web:91]
  • Medium‑term (1–2 years): If herd rebuilding stays slow, calf and feeder prices are likely to remain above long‑term averages, but eventual expansion and heavier placements will cap rallies and increase volatility. [web:53][web:91]
